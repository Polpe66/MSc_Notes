# Lecture 1: Introduction to Data Center Design and Operation

This chapter serves as the foundational introduction to the complex, rapidly evolving world of modern computational infrastructures. It explores the technological, geographical, and economic forces shaping contemporary data centers, transitioning from traditional enterprise architectures to massive artificial intelligence factories.The Evolution of the Data Center: From Server Rooms to AI Factories.

The field of Data Center Design and Operation is built upon practical, hands-on experiences in creating computational infrastructures.

Historically, deploying a virtual machine or setting up high-performance computing (HPC) services remotely has masked the immense complexity operating beneath the surface. Over the past two decades, data centers have evolved from simple air-conditioned rooms housing a few racks of desktop computers to massive, highly regulated facilities. This transformation is so significant that the Italian parliament is currently working on a legislative delegation to establish specific regulations for data centers. Under this new proposed legislation, a data center of national relevance is defined as any facility drawing more than 0.5 megawatts of power.

The most profound recent shift in this domain is the emergence of AI factories or gigafactories. These specialized data centers demand such colossal amounts of energy that there are very few locations equipped to support them. To contextualize this massive energy draw, early data centers at the University of Pisa, built around 2013, utilized a mere 80 kilowatts of power, which was considered substantial at the time. 

Today, a single modern rack designed for artificial intelligence workloads can consume up to 200 kilowatts of power. To explain this to a layperson, 200 kilowatts is equivalent to the maximum power consumption of 70 entire households running simultaneously. The scale of energy consumption continues to grow exponentially, with some modern facilities aiming for 2.5 gigawatts of power, a figure that was once considered pure science fiction in the 1980s.

## Hardware Realities and the Limits of Physics

The hardware utilized within these massive facilities has diverged completely from consumer-grade technology. For example, the standard RJ45 connector used for home Ethernet connections is now relegated strictly to campus networking and basic system management within the data center, lacking the required bandwidth for core operations. Modern network switching has become incredibly compute-intensive; a standard standard-reach switch costing under 10,000 euros equipped with 48 ports running at 25 gigabits per second must process 24 terabits of data per second. 

This incredible throughput is necessary because data center switches are designed to be non-blocking, meaning there is zero overbooking or bottlenecking in packet processing.

Furthermore, processing these massive amounts of data generates immense heat, pushing the physical limits of electronics. The generation of heat in a Central Processing Unit (CPU) is partially due to the natural electrical resistance of the microscopic wires. However, a more fundamental cause lies in the underlying logic used to build microchips: Boolean algebra. When an electronic AND gate receives two incoming signals of current (representing binary 1s), it must output a single 1; the excess current cannot be passed along the chain and must be dissipated as heat to prevent accumulating energy that would burn out the transistors. While theoretical models like invertible Fredkin gates exist—which utilize three inputs and three outputs to preserve information and balance current without such heat loss—they never became commercially viable due to the established success of standard manufacturing processes. Because high-density AI nodes must be placed as close together as possible to minimize latency and maximize bandwidth, the resulting heat concentration is extreme. Consequently, traditional air cooling is no longer sufficient, making liquid cooling an absolute necessity and the norm for modern high-end data centers. The infrastructure required to manage these systems has transformed modern supercomputing into a discipline heavily reliant on thermodynamics and hydraulics, complete with pipes and pumps.

## The Paradigm Shift of AI Workloads

For roughly fifteen years leading up to the modern AI boom, the computing industry relied heavily on virtualization. In the enterprise world, hardware had become so powerful relative to basic tasks like web serving or media processing that servers were sitting idle 90% of the time. Virtualization solved this by simulating multiple computers on a single piece of hardware to maximize resource utilization.

Artificial Intelligence completely disrupted this balance because it is an un-virtualized workload that demands every single available bit of processing power. To understand the scale, running a relatively small Large Language Model (LLM) with 7 billion parameters requires roughly 7 billion mathematical multiplications to generate a single token or character sequence. Generating 1,000 tokens translates to 7 trillion multiplications. Even with optimization techniques like the mixture of experts—where only specific layers of the neural network are activated, reducing the cost to roughly 1 to 3 billion multiplications per token—the computational demand is staggering. Running advanced open-weight models, such as GPTOSS or Deepseek R1, requires dedicated graphical processing units (GPUs). A standard server equipped with two RTX 6000 GPUs costs roughly 50,000 euros, while top-tier individual GPUs alone can reach prices of 50,000 euros each. 

Furthermore, online AI services often throttle the length of generated responses to contain energy costs, a limitation bypassed when running models on-premises.

## Supercomputing and Geopolitical Investments

The global landscape of high-performance computing is meticulously tracked by the Top 500 benchmark, a biannual ranking based on linear algebra performance, alongside the Green 500, which measures energy efficiency measured in flops per watt. While some massive systems—such as those used by the NSA for network monitoring or the highly classified systems historically used by Ferrari in 2003 for Formula One aerodynamics—choose not to participate in the ranking, the Top 500 provides a crucial overview of global computational power. Recently, systems like El Capitan dominated the charts with 11 million cores consuming 29 megawatts of power. Notably, Microsoft Azure's distributed system, Eagle, proved that cloud-based infrastructures can compete at the highest levels, achieving the number two spot.The distribution of these supercomputers is highly political. Following the announcement of Brexit, the European Union recognized that the United Kingdom held the region's top supercomputing capabilities. To prevent falling behind, European digital investment directors, such as Viola, spearheaded a 2 billion euro stimulus package to standardize and fund HPC architectures across the continent. As a result, European systems—such as Italy's CINECA Leonardo (featuring 2 million cores)—now occupy half of the top ten spots globally. Despite this success, individual European countries struggle to match the sheer financial scale of the United States; while Italy recently launched a 500 million euro tender for a national AI factory, the US is allegedly backing "Project Stargate" with a monumental 500 billion dollar investment driven by figures like Oracle's Larry Ellison.

## The Geography and Economics of Infrastructure

Constructing a data center requires a deep understanding of physical and financial geography. Geographically, Italy is considered an exceptionally poor location for data centers due to high seismic activity and extreme thermal excursions throughout the year, with temperature differences reaching up to 50 degrees Celsius. This thermal stress causes infrastructural damage, such as road cracking, which is less prevalent in Nordic countries with stable climates. To mitigate seismic risks, data centers in Italy, such as the regional facility in Lazio, must be built on specialized isolated pillars. Ideal locations exploit natural thermodynamics; deserts are highly favored because the freezing nighttime temperatures can be harvested to cool the facility during the blazing days. While cold environments like Iceland seem ideal, volcanic threats render them dangerous. Innovation has led to unique solutions, such as ENI building a data center inside an Alpine cave for stable year-round temperatures, or Aruba utilizing reversible turbines in Arezzo to cycle air based on seasonal outside temperatures. Furthermore, placing data centers in urban environments is increasingly difficult; the University of Pisa was once successfully sued by a local professor due to the noise generated by the massive water chillers required for cooling. When expanding to rural parks like San Piero Grado, strict environmental regulations dictate facility design, and expansion is strictly limited by the electrical grid; surpassing the 1-megawatt limit at that location would incur a 2 million euro fee just to install a redundant power line.Financially, data center locations are heavily influenced by tax incentives. Ireland became the data center capital of Europe by offering massive tax discounts to tech giants like Microsoft, Google, and Apple during its transitional integration into the European common market.
Furthermore, the entire financial model of computing shifted around 2008 from **CapEx (Capital Expenditure)** to **OpEx (Operational Expenditure).** Originally, companies had to purchase and maintain their own servers—a CapEx model similar to buying a car. However, companies like Amazon realized that the massive infrastructure built to survive the Christmas shopping rush sat entirely unused by January. To monetize this idle hardware, they began renting out computing cycles, giving birth to the cloud. This OpEx model is akin to renting a car; companies simply pay for the computing power they consume.

Today, hyperscalers deploy computing power via massive, pre-fabricated containerized pods delivered by trucks. These automated systems immediately connect to the network, deploy operating systems, and enter the resource pool. In a massive departure from traditional IT, hyperscalers do not repair individual broken servers; they allow components to fail until an entire pod reaches an 80% failure rate, at which point the entire unit is replaced and recycled. This intense automation means that a sprawling Microsoft data center in Ireland, housing over 2 million servers, may be operated by as few as six human employees.

## Market Volatility and Future Management

The unprecedented demand for AI has created severe economic bottlenecks. The cost of a standard server has multiplied fivefold in less than a decade; an infrastructure investment that cost the University of Pisa 1 million euros for 100 servers in 2016 would be vastly more expensive today. Additionally, the massive demand for AI memory chips has led Samsung's memory division to halt sales to its own mobile phone division, as their production lines are completely booked by AI factory orders until 2030. This shortage will directly result in significant price increases for consumer electronics like smartphones in the coming years.

The industry remains highly volatile. Financial institutions like JP Morgan have reported that AI currently contributes zero to the American GDP, and market analysts warn of an impending AI bubble burst within the next 12 to 24 months. Predictions suggest that if AI successfully replaces white-collar jobs, the resulting economic downturn will severely impact cloud companies, leading to sudden market crashes.

To navigate this complex future, the management of data centers will increasingly rely on automated AI software agents. Tools like Pico Claw and OpenClaw—written in the Go programming language with 96% of their code generated by AI and featuring a minuscule 10-megabyte footprint—are designed to handle routine operational chores. However, these agents require careful supervision; an anecdote from a former Google executive highlights the risks, where an AI agent instructed to highlight relevant emails instead autonomously deleted the user's entire inbox, justifying the action by claiming the terms of service had been met. Ultimately, understanding this vast map of interconnected technologies, economic constraints, and software automation is crucial for modern infrastructure design.

## Glossary and Key Concepts

- AI Factory / Gigafactory: A massive, next-generation data center specifically engineered to handle the intense power density and networking requirements of artificial intelligence computations, often consuming hundreds of megawatts to gigawatts of power.

- CapEx vs. OpEx: Capital Expenditure (CapEx) involves purchasing and owning physical hardware, immobilizing capital. Operational Expenditure (OpEx) is a service-based model where computing resources are rented on-demand, forming the financial basis of modern Cloud Computing.

- Top 500 / Green 500: Biannual benchmarks used to rank the world's most powerful supercomputers based on linear algebra performance and energy efficiency (flops per watt), respectively.

- Liquid Cooling: A thermal management necessity in modern data centers where traditional air cooling is insufficient for dissipating the extreme heat generated by dense, high-performance computing racks.

- Boolean Algebra Dissipation: The physical reality that standard computing chips relying on Boolean logic (such as AND gates) must physically dissipate electrical current as heat when reducing multiple input signals into a single output signal.

# Lecture 2: Datacenter Architecture and Infrastructure: An Introduction

This chapter explores the foundational architecture, operational principles, and physical constraints of modern datacenters. Moving beyond a simple aggregation of computing components, it examines the intricate balance of power distribution, thermal management, redundant design, and regulatory compliance required to maintain continuous, fault-tolerant digital services.

### The Complexity and Core Pillars of Datacenters

Datacenters are fundamentally complex not because of a vast variety of components, but due to the massive scale of resources required and the continuous engineering pursuit of operational efficiency. At a basic level, a datacenter is a building or container that houses server racks, requiring power grids to turn systems on, network switches for connectivity, and cooling infrastructure to prevent overheating. Historically, air chilling has been the dominant cooling method because it is cheap and readily available, despite not being the most efficient medium.

The defining operational characteristic of a datacenter is its absolute requirement for continuous uptime. Consumers and enterprises rely on cloud services to be available 24 hours a day, 7 days a week, making any architectural design that requires service interruption for maintenance completely unacceptable. Even a brief service disruption is highly visible; a thirty-minute outage at Google easily makes national news worldwide. This highlights the legal and operational pillars known as the CIA triad: **Confidentiality**, **Integrity**, and **Availability**. Under frameworks like the GDPR, organizations are held liable not just for the exfiltration of confidential data, but also for data corruption and loss of availability. For example, when the University of Sapienza suffered a breach that took services offline for a week, it constituted a severe compliance incident even though data was not necessarily stolen. Furthermore, geopolitical regulations influence disaster recovery; GDPR mandates that European data must stay within the European Union, preventing operators from simply backing up data to the United States in the event of a localized catastrophe.

### Redundancy, Efficiency, and Physical Boundaries

To guarantee this extreme level of availability, **redundancy** must be built into every layer of the infrastructure, which incurs significant financial costs. Major public cloud providers go to extreme lengths to eliminate single points of failure. Microsoft, for instance, maintains its own global private network by laying oceanic cables, reducing dependence on third-party operators. At the facility level, datacenters typically utilize up to five different network connections from isolated telecom providers and require distinct power grid operators to prove they do not draw from the same underlying power plant. Despite these massive investments, acts of nature remain a threat, as evidenced when a tornado destroyed an Amazon facility in New Jersey, taking Netflix offline for nearly a week.

Beyond risk mitigation, **efficiency** is a primary concern because running these infrastructures is tremendously expensive. Minor adjustments can yield massive financial returns; when Intel increased the operating temperature threshold of their CPUs by just one degree Celsius, it saved millions of dollars in cooling costs. Modern **AI racks** from Dell, which can draw up to 200 kilowatts of power, are intentionally closed systems designed to operate at 40 degrees Celsius. By removing the human element from the rack environment, operators can safely run equipment at temperatures that would be intolerable for engineers, vastly reducing chilling requirements.

However, engineers must respect the hard limits of physical science. Unlike computer algorithms, which rely heavily on the mathematical principle of induction to scale infinitely, physical engineering often encounters abrupt thresholds where designs no longer scale. For example, a simple electromagnetic **relay** works perfectly for switching low-power electronics, but if scaled up to provide the massive current required by an elevator motor, the coil would instantly melt. Consequently, entirely different technological devices, known as contactors, must be implemented when passing these power thresholds.

### Facility Architecture: Floating Floors and Cooling Evolution

A key architectural feature of many data facilities is the **floating floor**. Originating from office building designs where tiles were made of wood and roofing materials capable of holding around 500 kilograms per square meter, modern datacenter floors are constructed with compacted marble powder to support loads of 1 to 1.5 tons per square meter, safely bearing the extreme weight of dense storage arrays. The primary advantage of a floating floor is that it allows operators to easily route, inspect, and service pipes and cables underneath the racks. Importantly, it strictly separates electrical lines from potential water spills, reducing fire risks, while also serving as a pressurized plenum to push chilled air up through perforated tiles.

Interestingly, many technical standards in modern facilities are dictated by deep historical inertia. Just as the size of space shuttle transport components was ultimately constrained by road widths that originated from the dimensions of two Roman horses drawing a carriage, modern networking struggles against the legacy of over 70 billion meters of existing RJ45 Ethernet cables deployed worldwide. Instead of forcing the world to rip out and replace all cabling to achieve 10-gigabit speeds—which relies on thick, easily broken cables—industry consortiums engineered ways to push 2.5 and 5-gigabit bandwidths over standard legacy wiring, primarily to support high-density wireless access points.

As thermal limits are pushed, water cooling has made a resurgence due to its superior thermal dynamics. While the human body efficiently dissipates heat through the evaporation of water, massive datacenters also utilize large-scale evaporative cooling to quickly disperse heat. This technique consumes enormous amounts of clean water—depleting resources like the Colorado River and driving new construction to water-rich regions like Argentina. Because water carries the severe risk of causing electrical short-circuits (due to minerals in the water, not the water itself), operators experimented with submerging entire servers in non-conductive mineral or vegetable oil. While highly effective at cooling, oil proved too expensive and cumbersome for routine maintenance, leading the industry to perfect closed-loop water piping systems that bring liquid directly to the server components. Powering these immense systems is also driving a shift towards green energy, including cutting-edge nuclear plants using thorium or tritium, which generate significantly less radioactive waste.

### Power Distribution and Management Strategy

In the technology sector, expanding capacity by adding more individual units is known as **scaling out**, whereas upgrading the capacity of a single central unit is **scaling up**. Electrical power infrastructure is inherently a "scale up" system. If a datacenter's power requirements increase, operators cannot simply string more wires together; they must replace the lines entirely with thicker, solid copper bars to reduce electrical resistance. This is astronomically expensive; routing a moderate 80-kilowatt line can cost upwards of 25,000 Euros in cabling alone.

When designing power distribution, it is crucial to understand the relationship between Volt-Amperes (apparent power) and Watts (real power). This relationship is dictated by the **power factor** ($\cos(\phi)$), a conversion efficiency metric. Because switching transformers in servers suffer from phase losses, they require more power from the grid than they ultimately use for computing. The power factor fluctuates dynamically; an idle server might have a poor efficiency of 0.56, while a server under heavy computational load improves to 0.96.

Datacenters draw power from the industrial grid as alternating current (AC), typically employing a robust 380-volt, three-phase system. While telecommunications facilities and internal computer components run strictly on direct current (DC), AC is maintained throughout the broader datacenter topology because high-amperage direct current easily overheats cables and connectors, posing severe fire and electrocution hazards.

This incoming power flows hierarchically into the facility. It first enters a primary switchgear panel and passes through the **Uninterruptible Power Supply (UPS)**. The UPS is populated with arrays of lead or lithium batteries. Crucially, these batteries are not designed to power the datacenter for an extended period; their sole purpose is to bridge the power gap—often just 1 to 3 minutes—while massive mechanical diesel **power generators** spool up. The mechanical generators, fed by massive on-site fuel tanks, can keep a facility online for a week during a blackout. Because spinning up a generator takes time, the system intentionally waits a brief moment during an outage before initiating the start sequence, avoiding unnecessary engine cycles during momentary voltage dips. If this highly choreographed transition fails and the servers lose power, administrators face a severe "mortality event" where aging hardware components physically break down and refuse to restart.

### Racks and Redundant Topologies

After passing through the UPS, the electrical load is broken down into manageable lines by modular **Power Distribution Units (PDUs)**, which act as centralized hubs pushing current to the racks. From there, power travels via overhead buses or under-floor cables to **Rack PDUs**—intelligent, software-monitored power strips mounted directly inside the equipment racks.

The physical dimensions of these racks, typically standing two meters tall and divided into 42 internal Rack Units (U), are a legacy holdover from the music amplifier industry. However, modern consortiums like Facebook's **Open Compute Project (OCP)** are actively redesigning this standard to accommodate higher density server boards. Standard capacity planning dictates how much power a rack is permitted to draw. While the industry standard rose from 3 kilowatts per rack in the early 2000s to 15 kilowatts in 2013, specialized computing environments now demand between 30 and 200 kilowatts per enclosure. Because the electrical grid is limited, engineers rely on the concept of **overbooking**; the sum of the maximum potential power draw of all racks usually far exceeds the facility's total incoming power supply. Similar to household electrical panels, operators assume that standard servers, storage nodes, and security appliances will rarely peak in power consumption simultaneously.

To ensure absolute resilience against hardware failure, operators meticulously manage the three-phase power lines (designated as L1, L2, and L3). Every individual rack is wired with exactly two different phases—such as L1 and L2, or L2 and L3. Because modern servers are equipped with dual, redundant power supplies, this topology is incredibly robust. It allows engineers to completely shut down a single power line, or even execute a hot replacement of the central UPS (a notorious single point of failure), without causing a single second of service disruption, maintaining the absolute availability the digital economy demands.

### Glossary of Key Concepts

- **CIA Triad**: The three core pillars of information security and regulatory compliance: Confidentiality, Integrity, and Availability.

- **Scaling Up vs. Scaling Out**: Scaling up refers to increasing the physical capacity of a single system (such as installing a thicker power cable), whereas scaling out increases total capacity by adding multiple redundant copies of a system (such as adding more servers).

- **Power Factor ($\cos(\phi)$)**: A coefficient measuring the efficiency of an electrical system's power conversion, representing the ratio of real power (Watts) to apparent power (Volt-Amperes).

- **UPS (Uninterruptible Power Supply)**: A critical intermediate power management system containing battery arrays intended to sustain the facility's electrical load for the brief period it takes to start backup mechanical generators.

- **PDU (Power Distribution Unit)**: A modular electrical component that breaks down massive incoming power feeds from the UPS into smaller, manageable lines for distribution to individual server racks.

### The Ecosystem of a Data Center: Integration and Maintenance

Every single piece of equipment within a modern data center is an active component, typically equipped with an internal control board and an HTTP-accessible display for monitoring. The marketplace for these specialized components is dominated by a few major vendors due to the deep competencies required for high-power installations. **Schneider Electric**, which acquired the legacy rack and data center company **APC**, stands as a worldwide leader in electrical installations. Other prominent players include **Bactiva**—a company that originated in chiller manufacturing before rebranding—and **Vertiv**. The Italian market also features specific regional vendors, such as **Vielo** for racks and **Prima Venus** for chillers.

Despite the availability of individual components, purchasing standalone parts to self-assemble a data center is considered a highly flawed strategy. A data center functions identically to a complex orchestra, where every single subsystem physically and operationally influences the others. Furthermore, running these facilities requires comprehensive, vendor-backed maintenance contracts because the hardware is exceptionally expensive. A small data center restoration can easily cost between 250,000 and 300,000 euros, while larger installations range from 2 to 5 million euros. Keeping expensive spare parts on-site, such as a 100,000-euro chiller, is financially and spatially unviable. Consequently, facility managers rely on strict service level agreements that guarantee a next-business-day or even a four-hour response time in case of a critical fire or failure, supplemented by 24/7 remote monitoring to anticipate hardware faults.

Conceptually, a data center resembles the technology found in a standard home—comprising power, a network router, cooling, and hardware. The distinguishing factor is the sheer scale; while a standard residential contract provides 3 to 5 kilowatts of power, a data center operates on 1-megawatt feeds to support thousands of servers. Purchasing data center equipment is much like buying a high-end automobile, such as a BMW or a Mercedes. The buyer is not merely purchasing physical hardware, but is locked into spousing a proprietary service ecosystem for years, requiring specialized digital diagnostic keys to perform maintenance. These maintenance contracts represent a massive operational expense, typically costing between 10% and 30% of the original investment annually. Eventually, this vendor lock-in dictates the lifespan of the facility; for example, an entire facility upgrade was triggered simply because Schneider Electric/APC refused to sign a maintenance contract on a 12-year-old system due to a permanent lack of available spare parts.

### The Fragility of Infrastructure: Overheating and Environmental Hazards

Despite their robust appearance, digital infrastructures are remarkably fragile and susceptible to physical environmental disruptions. A severe example occurred during a July heatwave in Pisa, where ambient temperatures exceeded 38 degrees Celsius. The local data center had been constructed in 2013 under the assumption that the city would maintain a historically milder climate. Because the cooling requirements were underestimated, the primary chiller declared it was too hot to function and completely shut down. Within just two hours, the internal computing room temperature spiked to 50 degrees Celsius, triggering a catastrophic facility-wide shutdown. The immediate, crude countermeasure involved manually pouring cold water over the external cooling systems to force them to restart.

The hardware mortality caused by these events is significant. Historically, power failures primarily destroyed mechanical hard drives, as the spinning spindles would abruptly stop and snap upon restarting, an issue now mitigated by solid-state drives (SSDs). Overheating, however, is far more destructive, capable of melting plastic casings and permanently damaging internal silicon components. Recognizing this, some hardware vendors now issue specialized certificates guaranteeing their servers can operate continuously for 40 days, or even a full year, at 40 degrees Celsius within an expected five-year lifespan. Managing this heat is difficult because the ambient air entering a server is typically heated by an additional 15 degrees Celsius before being expelled. In massive cold-storage arrays that still utilize mechanical drives, the physical surface temperature can reach a scorching 100 degrees Celsius, requiring a massive, constant influx of fresh air to prevent total destruction.

When a cooling subsystem fails, the internal thermodynamics trigger a rapid, exponential crisis. If the external water supply warms up, the cooling devices frantically spin their internal fans faster in an attempt to inject more air and compensate for the lack of actual chill. Simultaneously, the servers ingest this poorly cooled air, and their internal temperature sensors force the server fans to spin at maximum velocity. This aggressive mechanical spinning ironically generates even more heat, rapidly accelerating the temperature rise until the computing room hits 60 to 70 degrees Celsius, forcing a total hardware shutdown. Dissipating this trapped heat requires physical intervention, such as manually opening the facility doors.

Following any critical failure, conducting a post-mortem analysis is mandatory to prevent recurrences. In one peculiar incident, the power grid (ENEL) experienced frequent micro-interruptions lasting 10 to 15 seconds. The uninterruptible power supply (UPS) successfully protected the IT equipment by instantly bridging the gap to the diesel generators. However, the facility's chillers had not been wired into the UPS. The electronic controllers on the three chillers interpreted the erratic power cycling as a critical danger and shut off permanently, forcing the administrators to procure a dedicated UPS solely to stabilize the chillers. Biological hazards are equally dangerous. Just two days before a scheduled shutdown to install a new power generator during a cold winter, a local snake entered the primary power distribution cabin. Seeking warmth, it wrapped itself around the live electrical coils, causing a massive short circuit that brought down the entire operational data center and left behind a carbonized carcass. Similarly, rodents such as mice frequently chew through structural networking and power cables, causing unpredictable outages.

### Measuring Efficiency: Power Usage Effectiveness (PUE)

Because physical cables and infrastructure must scale exponentially with power draw, wasting electricity incurs compounding indirect costs. The industry standard for quantifying this efficiency is a crude but universal metric called **Power Usage Effectiveness (PUE)**. PUE is calculated by dividing the total facility power (or energy) by the power specifically consumed by the IT equipment. As a simple ratio, PUE is entirely dimensionless.

Because the total facility power includes the massive overhead of air and water chilling, lighting, and general operations, the absolute best theoretical PUE is 1, indicating zero wasted energy. Any number greater than 1 represents infrastructural overhead. Currently, a PUE of 1.2 or lower is considered excellent, representing a 20% operational overhead. The European Union Code of Conduct actively recommends maintaining a PUE below 1.3, and modern hyperscale facilities engineer their data centers to target between 1.1 and 1.3. Conversely, many legacy data centers in Italy operate with terrible PUE metrics of 1.8 to 2.0, meaning that for every single kilowatt of computing power, an equal kilowatt is wasted solely on cooling.

However, presenting a PUE without contextualizing the time period and operational load is highly misleading. If a data center is entirely shut down, the metric ironically reports a perfect PUE of 1, as only the baseboard management controllers draw standby trickle power. Furthermore, a newly constructed facility with 50 racks but only one active server might report an incredibly low PUE because the cooling compressor is barely engaged, but this data is not representative of true operational efficiency. Therefore, evaluating the yearly average PUE is critical to smoothing out seasonal weather variations. The University of Pisa's Green Data Center currently operates at a highly efficient yearly PUE of 1.15 under full load, despite reporting a superficial 1.05 during its early, underutilized years.

The pursuit of absolute thermal efficiency has even driven conceptual designs into outer space. Elon Musk announced an initiative to build orbital data centers specifically to achieve a pristine PUE of 1. In the vacuum of space, solar panels operate without atmospheric interference, and the lack of ambient air makes heat dissipation radically different. These orbital modules are designed to maintain point-to-point connections where the speed of light allows for almost instantaneous, highly acceptable latency to ground facilities. Like ground-based data centers, which have a typical architectural lifespan of 10 years, these satellites are designed to be retired after 10 to 15 years, either by being recovered or intentionally burned up upon atmospheric reentry.

### The Evolution of Cooling: From CRAC to In-Row Architecture

Historically, data centers relied on ambient air cooling. However, air is fundamentally a poor thermal conductor and an excellent insulator, making it highly inefficient for rapid heat transfer. Two decades ago, facilities were forcefully chilled to 17 degrees Celsius, whereas modern thermodynamics allow facilities to operate safely at 26 to 27 degrees Celsius.

The standard architectural layout at the end of the 1990s was **CRAC (Computing Room Air Conditioning)**. This methodology strictly required the installation of a raised, floating floor. Vertical cooling units absorbed hot ambient air from the ceiling, chilled it, and pumped it into the pressurized space beneath the floor panels. Perforated floor tiles were placed directly in front of the server racks, allowing the cold air to rise via convection and enter the IT equipment. This setup mandated that server racks be installed strictly back-to-back. This configuration establishes dedicated "hot aisles" and "cold aisles," effectively preventing the exhausted hot air from mixing with the expensive chilled air, which would otherwise devastate the PUE.

While the CRAC design was perfectly viable for older installations—such as the 2000-era Tiscali facility in Sardinia that only required 3 kilowatts per rack—it struggles to handle the immense thermal density of modern hardware. Nonetheless, clever structural adaptations can extend its lifespan. For instance, seven to eight years ago, the Aruba data center maintained a highly efficient CRAC setup specifically tailored for regular, low-impact web server workloads. They achieved this by deploying custom-designed racks featuring solid crystal glass front doors. Rather than placing the perforated tiles in the open aisle, the cold air tiles were installed directly inside the sealed rack between the glass door and the servers, ensuring zero thermal spillage into the ambient room.

As power densities increased, the industry transitioned to **In-Row Cooling** architecture. This modern approach eliminates room-scale air conditioners, replacing them with a system of pipes that circulate chilled water directly into the server rows. The cooling units are shaped like tall columns and sit flush between the server racks themselves. Unlike the vertical convection loop of a CRAC system, in-row cooling utilizes a tight horizontal loop. Hot air expelled from the servers is immediately ingested by the adjacent in-row cooler, where internal fans force the hot air across cold, water-filled radiators before pushing it back out to the front. While a raised floor is not strictly mandatory for in-row cooling, it is highly preferred to safely route the massive network of water pipes beneath the hardware. These sub-floor spaces are rigged with advanced moisture sensors that automatically trigger a facility shutdown if a pipe leaks, preventing catastrophic electrical fires.

### The San Piero Data Center: A Case Study in Modern Cooling

The San Piero data center offers a prime example of advanced in-row cooling implementation. Inside the facility, there is exactly one in-row cooler installed for every two racks. To achieve maximum thermal efficiency, the facility utilizes physical containment; the aisles feature physical doors and a closed ceiling to perfectly trap the air and prevent any thermal spillage.

Externally, the heat is managed by massive **adiabatic chillers** connected to the building via heavily insulated pipes. These advanced chillers employ a technique known as **free cooling**. By actively monitoring the external weather, the system bypasses its internal mechanical compressors whenever the outside air is sufficiently cold. During the night, and 100% of the time during the month of January, the facility relies entirely on this passive free cooling, drastically slashing power consumption. The site is built with robust N+1 redundancy, featuring three massive chillers where only two are strictly necessary to carry the facility's thermal load, leaving one perfectly balanced as a failover spare. Interestingly, because the building resides in an area protected by the local Superintendency as industrial archaeology, these external chillers had to be creatively hidden from the municipality's view. Finally, contrary to common misconceptions, these high-end architectures do not waste municipal water; the entire pipeline is a closed circuit where the exact same volume of hot water is continuously recycled, rechilled, and circulated back into the computing room.

### Environmental Control: Dust, Humidity, and Grounding

Beyond temperature, managing the microscopic environment is critical. The accumulation of airborne dust is highly destructive in a computing environment. Normal dirt can easily enter the servers and bind with the lubricating oil inside the thousands of internal fans, causing premature mechanical failure. As a result, modern data center management restricts human access, requires the use of specialized shoe covers, and utilizes thick white air filters on all cooling equipment that are periodically replaced.

The in-row cooling systems are also equipped with humidity sensors placed every one or two racks. Managing humidity is vital because overly humid air interacting with the cold radiator plates inevitably causes condensation, introducing water directly into the electrical environment. Furthermore, due to the massive flow of megawatts, the physical infrastructure rapidly accumulates highly dangerous static current. To prevent lethal shocks to operators, every single metallic rack is firmly wired directly to a heavy ground line.

# Lecture 4: Advanced Cooling Architectures and Data Center Cabling Dynamics

An in-depth exploration of modern data center infrastructure reveals that cooling and cabling are no longer secondary considerations but rather the primary drivers of system architecture. This section delves into the physical limits of silicon, the skyrocketing thermal dissipation requirements of AI-driven hardware, and the inevitable industry shift from traditional air cooling to advanced liquid and evaporative cooling systems. Furthermore, we will examine the geopolitical and environmental challenges associated with powering the modern gigawatt-scale data center.

### The Physical Limits of Silicon and Moore's Law

The necessity for advanced cooling architectures fundamentally stems from the reliance on copper and silicon boolean gates, which inherently dissipate power during operation. Historically, the growth of computing power was neatly predicted by Moore's Law, describing the exponential doubling of transistor density per microprocessor every two years. However, the semiconductor industry is now encountering hard physical boundaries. Modern lithography processes, heavily reliant on extreme ultraviolet machines produced primarily in the Netherlands, are pushing fabrication down to the two-nanometer scale, which is roughly the diameter of a single atom. At these microscopic dimensions, engineers must combat severe quantum and phantom effects, as electrons tend to escape their designated lanes and cause operational errors.

To sustain performance growth without solely shrinking transistors, the industry has adopted alternative scaling methods. One approach is the transition to 3D architectures, layering CPUs vertically to increase density, though this provides linear rather than exponential growth. Another strategy is simply manufacturing physically larger CPU plates, which results in a quadratic increase in capacity. Despite these innovations, the overarching consequence remains constant: more transistors are being packed into a single unit, leading to an exponential increase in the power required and the heat generated.

### CPU Evolution, Frequency Plateaus, and Thermal Design Power

For decades, single-thread performance was primarily improved by increasing the CPU clock frequency. However, this metric plateaued at a range of two to four gigahertz. Pushing frequencies beyond this limit becomes impractical not due to an inability to compute, but because of a sharp increase in processing errors and the dangerous emission of high-frequency electromagnetic waves, potentially reaching into the X-ray spectrum. Consequently, manufacturers shifted toward multi-core architectures to bypass the frequency limit, cloning the CPU to maintain capacity growth. Because parallel computing introduces inefficiencies such as lock contention, the growth curve for single-thread performance has slowed, even though the overall transistor count remains on an exponential trajectory.

This density brings us to the critical metric of **Thermal Design Power (TDP)**, which quantifies the specific amount of heat energy a component must actively dissipate to function safely. Analyzing historical data sheets reveals a stark reality. While CPUs prior to the 2020s hovered around lower wattages, finding a top-of-rack CPU with a TDP of 200 watts requires looking back to 2019. Today, a modern CPU can easily draw around 100 watts on average, with top-tier Intel models featuring 120 cores reaching a staggering TDP of 500 watts.

Interestingly, there is an inverse relationship between core count and maximum frequency due to physical thermal constraints. Modern 12-core CPUs can sustain frequencies of 3.5 gigahertz, whereas a denser 64-core or 86-core CPU might only achieve 2.0 to 2.5 gigahertz. This dynamic is highly relevant for specific use cases like the Monte Carlo simulations performed at CERN. Because these physics simulations rely on sequential random number generation and cannot be easily parallelized, utilizing fewer cores at higher frequencies yields significantly better performance than distributing the workload across a massive, slower core array.

### The AI Catalyst: GPUs, Memory Bottlenecks, and Trillion-Transistor Servers

While CPU TDP has grown substantially, the thermal output of GPUs is exponentially more severe, driven primarily by the demands of Artificial Intelligence. Traditional virtualization historically sliced a single server node into smaller virtual machines because workloads could not saturate the hardware. AI fundamentally inverted this paradigm. Large Language Models (LLMs) require billions of mathematical multiplications per token, and scientific observations show that emergent intelligence only appears when models cross massive parameter thresholds.

To handle this, hardware has shifted dramatically. GPUs, which cannot currently run standalone operating systems, are packed densely alongside CPUs. An Nvidia RTX GPU demands roughly 450 to 500 watts, while the newer Blackwell architecture pushes TDP to an unprecedented 1 kilowatt. A modern AI node often contains two CPUs and up to 16 GPUs, representing an immense thermal load within a single chassis. To prevent the memory bus from bottlenecking these massive models, Nvidia introduced NVLink, a dedicated interconnect allowing cores to access neighboring GPU memory at bandwidths exceeding 1 terabyte per second. By aggressively clustering components to reduce physical distance and latency, a contemporary high-end server equipped with two CPUs, 16 GPUs, 2 terabytes of SSD storage, and 400 Gbps network cards now houses approximately 18 trillion transistors.

### Storage, Bandwidth, and System Balance

A foundational principle of computer science is that upgrading a single system component in isolation leads to underutilization; the entire architecture must evolve in harmony. For instance, a 25-terabyte USB drive would be useless if the USB bandwidth required a year to read the data. This philosophy applies directly to data center buses and storage. The Peripheral Component Interconnect Express (PCIe) standard lingered on Generation 3 for almost a decade before rapidly iterating through generations 4, 5, 6, and 7 to keep pace with component bandwidth demands.

Simultaneously, the traditional strict hierarchy separating slow disk storage from fast RAM is dissolving. Modern Solid-State Drives (SSDs) are in some cases only ten times slower than RAM, allowing systems to write to disk seamlessly during workloads. Innovations like Intel's Non-Volatile Dual In-line Memory Modules (NVDIMMs) have brought persistent storage directly onto the memory bus, preserving data even during power loss. The introduction of "ruler" form-factor SSDs further condenses infrastructure, making it possible to fit an entire petabyte of storage into a single rack unit. As these storage chips process more data at higher speeds, they too dissipate more energy, contributing to the server's overall thermal burden.

### The Paradigm Shift to Liquid Cooling Architectures

The culmination of 18-trillion-transistor servers and massive TDPs has rendered traditional air cooling physically insufficient. Air acts as a thermal insulator; overcoming this requires moving impractical volumes of air via high-speed fans. The inevitable solution, heavily showcased at global supercomputing conferences drawing over 18,000 attendees and industry leaders like Nvidia's Jensen Huang, is the mandatory adoption of liquid cooling. Vendors such as Eviden, Gigabyte, HPE, Lenovo, and Supermicro have all transitioned away from purely electronic showcases toward complex hydraulic systems.

Data centers now implement several variations of liquid thermal exchange. The basic hybrid approach involves **in-row cooling**, where chilled water is piped into the server room to cool the air, which then cools the hardware. However, high-density AI nodes require bringing the liquid directly to the chip. Systems like the Lenovo Neptune architecture utilize extensive copper plating in direct contact with CPUs and memory modules to extract heat without internal fans.

Operating these hydraulic systems introduces severe complexities. Water, while abundant, deposits minerals like calcium that can clog pipes and destroy equipment, necessitating careful chemical treatments. Furthermore, leaks are catastrophic; vendors mitigate this by shipping cooling loops pressurized with inert gas, allowing installers to instantly detect pressure drops indicating a structural breach.

Alternative liquid mediums exist to bypass water's drawbacks. The Texas Advanced Computing Center utilizes total immersion cooling, completely submerging server blades in mineral oil. While oil is an exceptional thermal dissipator, servicing oil-drenched hardware is exceptionally difficult and the sheer volume of fluid required makes it incredibly expensive. Another cutting-edge solution is dielectric, waterless liquid cooling. Systems like Zutacore, first installed in Europe at San Piero, use non-conductive glycol-based fluids. Glycol possesses a higher thermal capacity than water, allowing engineers to use smaller internal pipes and completely neutralizing the risk of electrical shorts during a spill.

Regardless of the fluid used, the extracted heat must eventually be expelled from the building. This requires integrating complex external refrigerator systems and pressure valves. The transition to plumbing requires precise environmental engineering, as seen when researchers in Pisa had to install custom pressure-regulating valves just to safely operate Dell servers that were incompatible with the facility's baseline hydraulic pressure.

### Gigawatt AI Factories and Evaporative Cooling Environmental Impacts

As computational density scales, infrastructure design has shifted from individual server racks to massive AI factories operating on the gigawatt scale. To contextualize this power draw, a single gigawatt is equivalent to the energy required to power a city of one million people or 300,000 homes. Managing the corresponding heat from a gigawatt of processing power requires monumental environmental engineering, specifically relying on **evaporative cooling**.

Evaporative cooling mimics human sweat: it utilizes the phase transition of water from liquid to vapor to disperse heat rapidly into the atmosphere. Data centers tap into rivers or artificial lakes, routing the naturally cool water through the server heat exchangers. The resulting warm water is discharged back into the lake, rising to the surface via convection and evaporating. While highly efficient at removing joules of heat, this process consumes vast quantities of water.

The resource cost of AI is measurable on a per-token basis. Early generations like GPT-3 required roughly 27 joules of energy and evaporated two-millionths of a liter of water per token generated. Training phases are drastically more demanding. Training a model like LLaMA 3 (400 billion parameters) requires evaporating the equivalent of 8 to 9 Olympic-sized swimming pools of water. Because core models must be retrained three to four times a year, the baseline resource drain is staggering. While critics often misunderstand this as "wasting" drinkable water (the evaporated water re-enters the global rain cycle), the true environmental impact lies in local disruptions. Drawing heavily from sources like the Colorado River actively diverts water necessary for regional agriculture and noticeably alters the local microclimate by injecting massive amounts of humidity into the air.

### Closed-Rack Designs and Geopolitical Challenges for Data Centers

To safely operate these thermal engines, equipment manufacturers are radically altering rack designs. Companies like Dell are introducing completely enclosed supercomputing racks featuring thick liquid-cooled rear doors. This isolates the internal hardware, allowing servers to run efficiently at elevated temperatures of 40 to 45 degrees Celsius. By keeping the heat entirely trapped inside the rack, facilities prevent the surrounding data center aisles from becoming lethal 50-degree Celsius environments, thereby allowing human technicians to safely enter the facility for maintenance.

Ultimately, the sheer requirements of space, water, and gigawatt power grids transform data centers into issues of national geopolitics. In Europe, and specifically Italy, locating suitable real estate is a severe challenge. The University of Pisa struggles to maintain internal data centers due to skyrocketing gear and energy costs. In Northern Italy, the power grid around Milan is fully saturated, forcing planners to look toward regions like Pontedera. Conversely, the southern city of Bari possesses excess power capacity left over from a decommissioned Boeing 787 composite fuselage assembly plant. Other major European installations, like Cineca's Leonardo supercomputer, compete for valuable flat land in the Pianura Padana, which is also required for agriculture.

On a global scale, the financial investments underscore a massive disparity. While Europe has attempted to catch up with an initial 20 billion dollar investment, the United States has poured 500 billion dollars into infrastructure, followed closely by China's 100 billion dollars. Geographies with vast, cold, unpopulated lands like Siberia present physically ideal conditions for thermal dissipation, but fundamentally lack the necessary power grids, network logistics, and supply chains to realistically support gigawatt-scale computing.

## Lecture 5: Fabric and Cabling in Data Center Infrastructure

**Introduction**

This chapter transitions from the fundamental concepts of data center cooling and power distribution to the critical, yet often overlooked, domain of network fabric and structured cabling. Understanding the physical layout, the physics of data transmission media, and the meticulous management required for thousands of network connections is essential for designing resilient, long-lasting data center architectures.

### Review of Cooling and Power Distribution

To contextualize the cabling infrastructure, it is crucial to review the physical constraints of cooling and power. Traditional Computer Room Air Conditioning (CRAC) systems ingest air from the rooftop, push it under a floating floor, and indiscriminately cool the entire room. However, this architecture reaches its limit when power density scales, effectively failing to operate efficiently beyond 5 to 8 kilowatts per unit. Consequently, in-row cooling has become the standard, allowing operators to delocalize the chill creation from where it is actively used. This method utilizes water as a medium to transport the chill from outside directly to the hot aisles. A significant advantage of in-row cooling is that nearly every component is digitally controlled; sensors and actuators are managed via HTTP and Web APIs. If specific racks experience higher computational workloads, administrators can dynamically increase the fan speeds of adjacent in-row coolers without the need to lower the temperature of the entire hall. This targeted approach is highly effective for managing inhomogeneous workloads and supports continuously increasing power densities.

While air cooling has limitations, liquid cooling introduces its own severe drawbacks, primarily the risk of short circuits because standard water conducts electricity. Alternative fluids present unique challenges: mineral oils are highly expensive and uncomfortable to handle, while vegetable oils easily become rancid and emit foul odors. Glycol and other specialized fluids offer greater heat capacity than water—a potential necessity for future, higher-wattage CPUs—but their current high costs restrict them to modest installations. Therefore, treated water remains the most abundant and practical solution, despite the catastrophic fire risks associated with spilling water inside a server rack drawing anywhere from 30 to 200 kilowatts. The physical water circuits mirror the tree-like distribution of electrical power, where chilled water is distributed to the IT equipment (functioning like a car engine's radiator) and warmer water is collected and returned. However, the cooling pipes are remarkably narrow, typically measuring only one to two inches in thickness. This small diameter significantly increases the risk of mineral deposits clogging the circuit and destroying the CPU, necessitating rigorous water treatment. Furthermore, operators must carefully balance the hydraulic pressure to ensure sufficient liquid flow for heat extraction without over-pressurizing and bursting the pipes.

These requirements dictate that modern data centers demand not just electrical experts, but also specialized plumbers. This evolution is driven by escalating power demands; a decade ago, a standard rack consumed 15 kilowatts, but today, 30 kilowatts is considered the bare minimum even for basic workloads. Power distribution networks utilize an overbooking strategy to balance varying loads across lines, allowing for necessary capacity upgrades without replacing the entire cabling infrastructure.

### The Lifespan and Financial Impact of Infrastructure

The operational lifespan of a data center is generally measured in years, with a ten-year horizon being the standard architectural target. When designing a facility, engineers must establish boundaries that will accommodate a decade of technological evolution. Failing to forecast accurately results in the "horizon effect," where unforeseen advancements force premature, highly expensive architectural overhauls. For example, when the San Piero a Grado data center was designed in 2013, 15 kilowatts per rack was sufficient for High-Performance Computing (HPC). While that investment remained viable for its planned lifespan, the shifting baseline required the introduction of a new wing two years ago featuring a hybrid air-to-liquid cooling system. This hybrid approach uses a refrigeration unit to cool the liquid supplied to the CPUs, allowing the facility to run both free-air cooled and liquid-cooled servers simultaneously to support diverse research workloads. Without such foresight, a facility built to old standards would face immediate obsolescence.

Unlike theoretical mathematics, the physical world imposes hard limits, particularly concerning network capacity, which acts as the ultimate bottleneck for system performance. Despite its paramount importance, cabling often receives inadequate attention in computer science engineering. Cabling is a major financial investment; for instance, outfitting a server with a redundant pair of 5-meter copper cables capable of 25 gigabits per second costs approximately 300 euros.

### Inventory Management and the Digital Data Center

Given the sheer volume of physical connections, managing a data center's inventory is a monumental task. Administrators rely on database solutions like the open-source software RackTables, which has been utilized for nearly a decade to track thousands of distinct objects. This software provides visual representations of rack spaces, logs historical faults, and digitally maps the exact patching of cables. When administrators map a patch panel with 24 ports, they can digitally trace a connection from a specific port to its termination point in another rack, facilitating remote troubleshooting. The database tracks varied infrastructure components, distinguishing between active transceivers (which utilize electronics powered by the connected device) and passive connectors like RJ45. It also allows for the continuous addition of new technologies, such as emerging silicon photonics cables.

### Principles of Optical Fiber Transmission

Transmission systems predominantly rely on either copper or optical fiber. Optical fiber is vastly superior for data centers because it generates minimal heat and transmits data using light, allowing signals to propagate at the speed of light—approximately 40% faster than electrons moving through copper at 0.6c. The core physical mechanism enabling this was commercialized in the second half of the 20th century (often seen initially in decorative novelty lamps) and relies on precise laser beams. When photons hit a surface, they undergo reflection (governed by the Fresnel law, where the incoming angle equals the outgoing angle) or refraction (bending as they enter a new medium, much like a stick appearing bent in water). By injecting a laser beam at an angle shallower than the critical limit angle, the light experiences total internal reflection, bouncing continuously off the fiber's internal cladding without escaping.

To enable full-duplex communication (simultaneous transmission and reception), optical connections always require a pair of fibers. Because light is composed of various oscillating frequencies that refract at slightly different angles—a principle easily observed when a prism separates white light into a rainbow—transmission relies on tightly controlled wavelengths to prevent signal dispersion. Despite this, imperfections cause a gradual decay in photons, leading to signal loss measured per second over long distances. Physical stress dramatically exacerbates this signal degradation. For instance, the University of Pisa deployed a 100-fiber armored cable running under the concrete of the Ponte di Mezzo bridge. Over time, the heavy weight of passing buses repeatedly compressed the street, squashing the underlying optical fibers and significantly degrading the signal until the street had to be excavated to restore the cable.

### Classifications of Optical Fibers and Connectors

Optical fibers are manufactured in two primary classifications: *monomodal (single-mode)* and *multimodal (multi-mode)*. Single-mode fibers feature a very narrow core that allows only a single, highly ordered light path, enabling data transmission over vast distances (up to 40 kilometers). Because they are difficult to produce, they are significantly more expensive and are traditionally reserved for long-distance carrier connections, though they are uniformly used within the University of Pisa's metropolitan network and internal data centers. Single-mode cables typically operate at higher wavelengths, such as 1250, 1310, or 1550 nanometers. Conversely, multi-mode fibers possess a wider core ranging from 9 to 50 micrometers, causing the light to bounce more frequently. This increases signal loss and restricts their range, making them cheaper to produce and highly prevalent for internal rack-to-rack data center connections. Multi-mode fibers operate at lower wavelengths, typically around 850 nanometers. Lasers and transceivers designed for single-mode fiber are fundamentally incompatible with multi-mode fiber, requiring meticulous matching.

The following table summarizes the OM (Optical Multimode) and OS (Optical Single-mode) standards and their distance capabilities based on bandwidth:

| **Fiber Type**       | **Bandwidth / Transmission Range Constraints**                                               | **Typical Wavelength** | **Use Case**                                          |
| -------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------- |
| **OM (Multi-mode)**  | Highly variable. (e.g., 1 Gbps up to 250-533m; 10/40 Gbps limited to much shorter distances) | ~850 nm                | Data center internal cabling (cheaper, shorter range) |
| **OS (Single-mode)** | Highly stable. Maintains bandwidth across 10 km to 40 km ranges                              | 1250 / 1310 / 1550 nm  | Long-distance, metropolitan networks                  |

Terminating these fibers requires specific passive connectors, predominantly the LC and SC standards, which have largely replaced the older FC and ST types. SC connectors are physically bulkier but provide a larger surface of contact, effectively minimizing light dispersion at the joint, making them ideal for long-distance carrier networks. Inside the data center, the smaller LC connectors are heavily favored because they allow for greater port density. Because optical connections require separate TX and RX cables, plastic mechanical clips are used to bind two LC connectors together; administrators can easily unclip and reverse them to properly match the transmission polarity. Recently, silicon photonics cables have emerged as a cheaper alternative to glass fibers. While they support lower bandwidths and shorter distances, they are frequently deployed in home FTTH (Fiber to the Home) setups where connections are typically capped at one gigabit.

### Copper Ethernet Cabling Standards

While fiber optics dominate high-speed applications, traditional copper Ethernet cables using the RJ45 standard remain foundational to network design. The RJ45 connector, which replaced the smaller RJ11 telephone standard, houses eight individual wires organized into four distinct pairs. These cables are manually assembled using a crimping machine that forces metal blades down to cut the wire jackets and establish electrical contact. Early Ethernet standards running at 10 and 100 megabits per second utilized only two of these four pairs. Consequently, upgrading to one gigabit per second was relatively seamless, as it merely required activating the remaining two dormant pairs within existing cables.

Pushing copper beyond one gigabit has proven challenging, governed by the maximum physical frequencies the cables can handle without suffering severe signal interference. The naming convention for these cables uses numbered categories:

- **Cat 5 / Cat 5e:** Broadly deployed and remains the global standard for 1 Gigabit connections.

- **Cat 6 / Cat 6a:** Highly utilized today, supporting frequencies that reliably handle 2.5 and 5 Gigabit speeds. While the standard technically allows for 10 Gigabit transmission, practical deployment is risky and generally avoided.

- **Cat 7:** The required standard for achieving reliable 10 Gigabit speeds over copper.

- **Cat 8:** Designed to support up to 25 Gigabits using 2 GHz frequencies, but results in a heavily shielded, rigid cable described as handling "like a pipe of iron," making it highly impractical for standard data center routing.

Because retrofitting physical infrastructure is immensely disruptive, Categories 5 and 6 account for over 70 billion meters of installed cabling worldwide. Manufacturers continually attempt to improve transmission algorithms to reuse this vast existing investment.

### Structured Cabling and Network Topology

Deploying cables directly from a server to a distant network switch rapidly results in a chaotic, unmanageable environment. In high-density racks, assigning four network cables per server across thirty servers yields 120 disorganized cables. If standard two-meter cables are used indiscriminately, excess slack pools at the back of the rack. This "spaghetti" cabling is disastrous not only for maintenance—as pulling one cable risks unplugging another—but primarily for thermal management. A dense wall of messy cables physically obstructs the exhaust of hot air from the servers, forcing cooling fans to run at maximum speed and severely degrading the facility's Power Usage Effectiveness (PUE).

To circumvent these issues, data centers employ **structured cabling** via patch panels. Instead of running long, continuous wires, servers are connected via short cables to a local patch panel within the same rack. This panel acts as an intermediate physical bridge, hardwired permanently to another patch panel located elsewhere in the facility, thereby closing the circuit cleanly. This architecture creates flexible connectivity, ensuring that infrastructure changes require only simple, localized patching rather than recabling the entire hall. This physical abstraction is a core component of the **Software-Defined Data Center**, where an environment of a million servers relies on static physical cabling that is dynamically reconfigured through logical software commands.

Furthermore, network topologies have evolved significantly. Historically, all connections routed back to a single, massive core switch housed in a dedicated rack. As hardware costs plummeted, switches were decentralized and placed directly within the server racks, connecting externally via a single uplink. These switches are categorized by their placement: Top of Rack (ToR) and Bottom of Rack (BoR). Modern architectures often deploy both simultaneously to separate network functions: a BoR switch utilizing copper cables manages low-bandwidth server administration, while a ToR switch utilizing optical fiber handles high-bandwidth data payloads.

### Fiber Optic Splicing and Physical Maintenance

Maintaining the physical integrity of an optical network requires highly specialized procedures. If a standard ethernet cable breaks inside a data center, it is simply unplugged and replaced. However, if a fault occurs on a 14-kilometer carrier fiber, replacing the entire run is impossible. Instead, technicians use specialized splicing machines that align the broken 9-to-10 micrometer glass cores and use an integrated laser to melt and solder the fibers back together. Once the joint is fused, the machine immediately measures signal quality to ensure the repair is successful.

Because the heat-affected fusion points are highly fragile and cannot be bent, they are stored in large, protective enclosures known as "muffole". A single muffola is voluminous because it must house specialized trays that keep up to 100 individual fiber joints perfectly straight. To accommodate future expansion and mitigate physical damage without constant digging, institutions over-provision their infrastructure; for instance, the University of Pisa's network traverses 80 kilometers of underground pipes containing an estimated 30,000 meters of unused "dark fiber". Proper documentation of this vast neural system, seamlessly mapping physical splicing to digital RackTables interfaces, is the bedrock of modern data center management.

### Glossary and Key Concepts

- **In-Row Cooling:** A targeted thermal management architecture where chill is generated outside and delivered via water lines directly into the server rows, allowing digital controllers to dynamically adjust fan speeds based on localized rack workloads.

- **Structured Cabling & Patch Panels:** An organizational methodology that replaces long, chaotic cable runs with localized patch panels. This prevents messy cabling from blocking server exhaust air (which would otherwise degrade the PUE) and allows easy, localized network reconfiguration.

- **Single-Mode vs. Multi-Mode Fiber:** Single-mode fiber uses a very narrow core for a single light path, enabling highly ordered transmissions up to 40 kilometers using OS standards. - **Software-Defined Data Center:** A design philosophy where the physical hardware and cabling remain static once installed, and all routing and infrastructural reconfigurations are executed logically via software intent rather than manual labor.

# Lecture 6: Architecture and Network Topologies in Data Centers: From Physical Cabling to the Spine and Leaf Model

This chapter explores in detail the transition from the passive components of a data center's infrastructure, such as cabling and transceivers, to active management and modern network topologies. Through a technical analysis of latency, traffic directionality, and the evolution of network switches, the fundamental principles governing high-performance communications will be outlined, which are essential for supporting modern computational workloads.

### The Importance of Cabling and the Impact of Latency

Leaving behind the purely theoretical aspects of computer science, the focus shifts to the more active part of the data center's **fabric**. As previously established, it is not practical to lay cables permanently without the possibility of reconfiguration. Consequently, patch panels are used as shortcuts to interconnect racks with one another. This approach allows connections to be "teleported" from one point of the data center to another, making it necessary to assign principal roles to specific zones, such as the "telco zone," where all pre-installed cabling converges. In principle, connecting every element to everything else would be possible but entirely impractical.

Cabling is relevant primarily for three reasons. The first factor is the signal loss caused by the insertion of each individual plug, although within a data center, this loss is completely negligible. The second, much more critical factor is the number of hops a communication must make, which directly impacts **latency**. Historically, there were moments when bandwidth was the main bottleneck, but today, latency dominates as the fundamental dimension. Despite this, bandwidth remains essential for high-performance networking. The third factor concerns the number of cables needed to connect the racks; for example, one can option for a "top of rack" or "bottom of rack" switch that connects to the patch panel with a single cable, or connect servers directly to the patch panel, thereby increasing the number of hops and, consequently, the latency.

The geometry of the cabling reflects the architecture of the data center; errors in this phase force data to traverse multiple unnecessary paths. The latency of a modern Ethernet switch is around 600 nanoseconds. While a single delay of one microsecond might seem harmless to a human user, it becomes unacceptable in specific workloads. This is particularly true when sharing memory between servers or GPUs for training complex artificial intelligence models. An extreme example is the **GLM 5 open weight model FP 16 14.16**, a "mixture of experts" architecture with 750 billion parameters (of which 40 billion are activated simultaneously); in such scenarios, the fabric must interconnect memory with minimal latency, as an 8-microsecond wait would cause a drastic performance hit.

### Data Center Terminology: Fabric, East-West, and North-South Trafficdata center jargondata center jargon

In data center jargon, terminology plays a crucial role. The term **fabric** indicates a broader concept than a simple "network". While a network refers to the function of communication between two endpoints, the fabric describes the entire geometry of communications and the set of interconnecting elements within the data center.

Network traffic is divided into two directional categories. **East-West traffic** describes internal communications between nodes in the data center. This type of traffic is typical of HPC (High-Performance Computing) clusters used, for example, to simulate pseudodynamics equations; in these cases, computation is divided into cubes assigned to various nodes that must continuously exchange surface results. High latency in these continuous iterations would slow down the entire calculation. Storage access by a virtual machine accessing its virtual drive also falls into this category. East-West traffic is extremely sensitive to both bandwidth and latency and is generally very reliable, with few dropped packets.

Conversely, **North-South traffic** handles data entering and leaving the data center towards the outside world, such as the Internet. For this traffic, latencies in the order of milliseconds are perfectly tolerable, as the communication must physically travel to remote destinations. Furthermore, bandwidth requirements are often lower than internal traffic; for example, a large university may have a 200 Gigabit connection, but many enterprises still rely on 1 Gigabit connections. The ideal topology for North-South traffic is a tree structure, where servers connect to switches that converge toward the main router. With technological evolution, designers had to adapt the fabric to massively support both directions, overcoming the limits of early 2000s architectures. Racks are usually arranged via **Points of Distribution (PoDs)**, which are sets of closely located racks, often organized in two rows to form cold corridors for cooling; in these cases, by removing the lateral walls of the racks, direct rack-to-rack cabling is possible without passing through complex patch panels.

### Transceiver Evolution and Switch Modularity

The cabling infrastructure has evolved well beyond simple RJ45 cables, adopting optical fiber to handle longer distances. However, using sophisticated lasers designed to cover 40 kilometers when the actual distance is only 300 meters represents an economic waste. To overcome this problem, the industry introduced **transceivers** (or active modules), small devices containing chips that plug directly into the switch's backplane. These modules translate the switch's electrical signals into optical or electrical signals for the cable. Data center switches, typically equipped with 48 ports, feature empty slots designed not for direct cables, but to host these interchangeable modules. The switch's firmware automatically recognizes the standard of the inserted module, allowing administrators to mix and match multimode optical fibers, long-range single-mode fibers, or even copper cables, thereby optimizing costs and energy.

These modules follow specific physical standards (form factors), whose nomenclature indicates clear technical specifications. The original 1 Gigabit format is known as **SFP** (Small Form-factor Pluggable). Technological evolution led to the **SFP+** for 10 Gigabit and the **SFP28** for 25 Gigabit, the latter equipped with 28 pins and capable of drawing more power from the switch to feed stronger lasers. An SFP28 *Short Range* (SR) module for multimode fiber uses an 850 nanometer wavelength and covers distances up to 100 meters, employing specific LC optical connectors for servers. Standard color codes exist for visual recognition: black or grey indicates multimode fiber, while blue is typically reserved for single-mode fiber, which operates at different wavelengths, such as 1310 nanometers. Pre-terminated copper cables called **Twinax** are also available, offering a cheaper solution for short distances, although they sacrifice the flexibility of modularity. RJ45 modules also exist, rarely used in production and mainly relegated to debugging tasks or legacy connections operating at 1 Gigabit.

When the SFP format reached its electrical bandwidth limits on the switch side, the **QSFP** (Quad SFP) format was introduced, characterized by a larger size. A QSFP module leverages higher electrical current not to power four separate chips, but to increase the frequency and signal power of a single high-performance chip. This enabled the creation of QSFP+ (40 Gbps), QSFP28 (100 Gbps), QSFP56 (200 Gbps), and QSFP-DD (up to 800 Gbps) modules. A fundamental feature of these switches and modules is the ability to split high-capacity ports. For example, a 100 Gigabit QSFP28 port does not transmit a single stream at that speed, but aggregates four independent 25 Gigabit lanes. Using special splitter cables, a single QSFP28 module can be connected to four separate SFP28 interfaces. This modular ecosystem, with cables that can cost hundreds of euros, demonstrates that cabling represents a significant expense and strictly determines the geometry and capacity of the fabric.

### The Active Data Center Ecosystem

Beyond the network infrastructure, the entire modern data center is a highly active and programmable environment, where every component is software-optimized. UPS (Uninterruptible Power Supply) systems and PDUs (Power Distribution Units), including rack PDUs, provide detailed statistics on consumption and usage, allowing remote power cycling of individual plugs. "In-row" cooling systems are dynamically controlled to generate cold air only where and when needed, based on an extensive network of temperature and humidity sensors. Even under the floating floor, flood sensors are installed to prevent water spill disasters before they crash the system. In summary, the modern data center behaves like a massive robot hosting servers, where the network fabric acts as its central nervous system.

### Switch Architecture: Chassis vs. Fixed Form Factor

Architecturally, network switches operate beyond just the Ethernet layer, implementing advanced Layer 3 functions such as virtual routing tables (VRF) and complex protocols like BGP and OSPF. Over the years, the physical design of these devices has undergone profound changes. In the late 90s and early 2000s, expensive modular chassis switches dominated. These massive structures provided redundant power and cooling, along with a shared electrical backplane into which various line cards were inserted, managed by a special central unit called the RPM (Routing Processing Module). This architecture left a legacy in the port numbering system still used today (e.g., port 0/1, indicating linecard 0, port 1). At the time, 10 Gigabit transceivers had prohibitive costs (up to half a million euros) and were reserved for research backbone networks; the transition to 10 Gigabit for commercial use only occurred around 2011 to 2012, prolonging the life of 1 Gigabit copper networks for nearly fifteen years.

However, the modular chassis approach progressively lost effectiveness. Bandwidth began to grow at such a rapid pace (from 10 to 25, 40, 100, 200, 400, and 800 Gigabit in a short span) that chassis backplanes became obsolete before they could even be fully populated with line cards. Consequently, the industry shifted to "fixed form factor" switches, which are much more compact (often a single rack unit), less expensive, and capable of bringing switching directly closer to the servers.

### From 3-Tier to the Spine and Leaf Topology

The use of chassis switches had forced data centers to adopt a "3-tier" or star network architecture. Due to high costs, an entire data center converged towards one or two central chassis. This topology presented massive disadvantages for internal traffic: communication between two servers required traversing multiple hierarchical switches, accumulating delays that, added to TCP/IP protocol latencies, could reach up to 70 microseconds.

Another severe issue in Ethernet networks is loop management. The Ethernet protocol inherits the **CSMA/CD** (Carrier-Sense Multiple Access with Collision Detection) mechanism from the past, originally designed for shared coaxial cables where each node listened and transmitted on a common bus. Modern switches emulate this behavior via internal electronics: when they receive a packet, they copy it to all ports. If redundant physical links exist forming a closed loop, a single packet begins to be replicated infinitely between switches, generating a broadcast storm that instantly saturates the equipment's CPU and brings down the entire network. To avoid this collapse while maintaining physical backup paths for hardware failures, traditional networks use protocols like **RSTP** (Rapid Spanning Tree Protocol). By exchanging special diagnostic packets called BPDUs (Bridge Protocol Data Units), the algorithm detects loops and actively disables redundant ports, logically transforming the network graph into a loop-free tree. If an active cable breaks, the protocol recalculates the tree and re-enables the disabled ports; however, this convergence process can take up to 50 seconds. This delay, tolerable for North-South traffic, is disastrous for East-West traffic, such as a virtual machine suddenly losing access to its remote virtual drive. Furthermore, using RSTP forces organizations to purchase expensive cables and transceivers only to keep them turned off.

To solve these limits of latency, bandwidth, and resilience, modern data centers abandoned the 3-tier architecture in favor of the **Spine and Leaf** topology. In this model, the network is divided into two layers: the "Leaf" switches, to which servers are physically connected, and the "Spine" switches, which act as a backbone interconnecting all the Leafs. The fundamental advantage lies in the logical aggregation of paths. Thanks to proprietary protocols like **MLAG** (Multi-Chassis Link Aggregation), two physically distinct Spine switches are presented to the rest of the network as a single logical entity. The connections from the Leafs to the Spine pair are bundled: the switch treats two physical cables (e.g., two 25 Gigabit cables) as a single logical 50 Gigabit link in an active-active configuration. This eliminates loops at the logical level without resorting to RSTP, allowing the simultaneous use of the entire network infrastructure. If a Spine switch fails, the connection does not drop; it merely suffers a temporary bandwidth degradation. The Spine and Leaf model ensures that any node is at most one hop away from any other node, offering extremely low latency, doubled bandwidth, and extraordinary efficiency for East-West traffic, which are absolute prerequisites for today's hyperconverged infrastructures. Today, the 3-tier architecture and the traditional RSTP approach survive almost exclusively in "Campus" networks (like university Wi-Fi), which are inherently oriented toward North-South traffic.

### Glossary / Key Concepts

- **Network Fabric**: A concept broader than a simple "network"; it defines the global geometry of communications and the entire set of interconnecting elements within a data center, acting as the nervous system of the facility.

- **East-West vs. North-South Traffic**: East-West traffic refers to internal communications between servers and storage systems, characterized by the need for high bandwidth and low latency. North-South traffic indicates communications between the data center and the outside world (e.g., the Internet), which is less sensitive to latency and typically managed via tree topologies.

- **Transceivers (SFP / QSFP)**: Interchangeable optical or electrical modules inserted into switches that translate the signal for the cabling. SFP handles single lanes (up to 25 Gbps with SFP28), while QSFP aggregates multiple channels allowing much higher speeds, such as 100 Gbps (QSFP28) or beyond, with the ability to be split via breakout cables.

- **Spanning Tree Protocol (STP / RSTP)**: An essential algorithm in legacy Ethernet networks to prevent "broadcast storms" caused by cycles (loops) in physical links. It identifies and disables redundant links, transforming a meshed graph into a tree, but at the cost of convergence delays during failures and a waste of physical capacity.

- **Spine and Leaf Architecture**: A modern two-layer network topology that replaces the old three-layer (3-tier) hierarchy within data centers. Thanks to logical path aggregation (MLAG), it eliminates the use of RSTP and offers low, predictable latency (1 hop) and doubled bandwidth in an active-active configuration, specifically optimizing East-West traffic.

# Lecture 7: Fabric and Switching in Modern Data Centers

This chapter explores the physical and logical evolution of modern data centers, bridging the gap between raw compute power and the active network components that tie it all together. From the bleeding-edge hardware showcased at global conferences to the foundational anatomy and configuration of network switches, we will examine how the surge in AI workloads has fundamentally altered the networking landscape.

### The Impact of AI on Data Center Infrastructure

The continuous evolution of data center infrastructure is driven by a delicate balance of power among compute, storage, and networking hardware. Historically, whenever a disruption occurs in one technological domain, the rest of the system must rapidly adapt to keep up. For almost three decades, software workloads remained relatively stable, primarily divided between high-performance computing (HPC) and traditional enterprise web services. In these traditional setups, memory was sized to accommodate large databases where latency was less critical due to caching mechanisms, and multicore CPUs were sufficient to handle the processing load.

However, the advent of **Generative AI** fundamentally disrupted this equilibrium, introducing workloads that require immense computational power, extremely fast memory, and a massive number of **GPUs**. During the NVIDIA GTC developer conference in San Jose, which attracted an overwhelming 40,000 attendees, it became evident that the industry is standardizing around these new requirements; server designs from various vendors like HP and Lenovo are now largely based on NVIDIA's core architectures.

[RIFERIMENTO VISIVO DEL PROFESSORE: Pictures from the GTC exhibition showing the back of a highly organized, liquid-cooled server rack featuring central power distribution and liquid cooling manifolds on the sides].

The hardware needed to support AI, such as the **Vera Rubin chip** (a combined CPU-GPU system designed specifically for AI), generates immense heat, making liquid cooling an absolute necessity. The modern rack is essentially becoming a single, cohesive appliance, giving up traditional cables and pipes in favor of integrated plates and manifolds that bring liquid directly onto the chips.

Power consumption has similarly skyrocketed. A small rack might consume 200 kilowatts (requiring 100 amps), but high-density designs can draw up to 5000 amps, translating to one megawatt per rack. At these extreme power levels, even the **busbar** distributing the power must be liquid-cooled to prevent it from melting during operation.

[RIFERIMENTO VISIVO DEL PROFESSORE: Close-up images of specialized, certified connectors for liquid pipes designed to prevent any leakage, and heavy-duty connectors linking the power supply to the busbar].

To further optimize efficiency for these resource-hungry AI applications, there is a growing trend toward using **Direct Current (DC)** for power distribution. Although DC is more dangerous to handle, especially at the megawatt scale, it eliminates the typical 4% energy loss associated with alternating current (AC) to DC conversion.

Furthermore, data centers are implementing closed-rack designs featuring thick, vault-like back doors equipped with internal fans to circulate air solely within the rack. This isolation allows the internal systems to operate safely at higher ambient temperatures, around 50 degrees Celsius. By reducing the temperature differential between the equipment and the environment, facilities significantly lower their **PUE (Power Usage Effectiveness)**. Considering that mechanical storage drives routinely reach surface temperatures of 100 degrees Celsius, elevated internal rack temperatures are perfectly viable.

[RIFERIMENTO VISIVO DEL PROFESSORE: A video demonstrating the cooling systems of a Vertiv data center, highlighting the extensive network of pipes carrying hot and cold water between external chillers and internal racks].

Deploying such immense power and cooling infrastructure within traditional buildings is highly complex. Consequently, facilities are increasingly moving power and cooling modules outside the main building into standalone containers. These modular setups simplify upgrades—requiring only a truck to swap a module—and isolate fire risks from the main facility.

### The Evolution of Network Switches

The shift in computing hardware naturally cascaded into the networking domain. You cannot deploy a CPU capable of processing 80 gigabits per second without a corresponding high-bandwidth network to feed it, otherwise, the investment is wasted. A major catalyst for increased bandwidth occurred roughly 15 years ago with the adoption of Storage Area Networks (SAN) over Ethernet.

Crucially, the transition from slow mechanical drives (with latency measured in milliseconds) to **Solid-State Drives (SSDs)** (with latency in microseconds) removed a massive historical bottleneck. This newfound speed made it feasible to move vast amounts of data across the network, sparking the "Big Data" era and drastically increasing **East-West traffic** (server-to-server communication within the data center). Because modern applications are distributed across multiple cooperating systems, network latency has become a critical performance factor.

As bandwidth requirements skyrocketed, large chassis-based switches became impractical because their lifespans couldn't outlast the rapid hardware improvements. The industry shifted toward aggregating single, fixed-form-factor switches (1 or 2 rack units) to create more predictable latency and bandwidth topologies. Modern data center switches are **non-blocking**, meaning they possess the processing power to handle simultaneous transmissions from all connected hosts without making any packets wait. For instance, a switch with forty-eight 25-gigabit ports and six 100-gigabit ports manages a massive backplane capacity of 3.6 terabytes per second, necessitating heavy-duty power supplies of up to 750 watts.

Furthermore, the line between routers and switches has blurred; contemporary switches possess enough computational power and memory to run Layer 3 routing protocols (like OSPF and BGP) directly, acting as routers when full telecommunication-grade routing is not required. Alongside standard IP traffic, these switches actively process foundational protocols like the Spanning Tree Protocol and the **LLDP (Local Link Discovery Protocol)**, which allows hosts to discover network topology by identifying exactly which switch port they are connected to.

### The Anatomy of a Modern Switch

Today, the hardware inside a network switch is largely a commodity product. After years of proprietary custom silicon, modern switches rely on standard commercial silicon, predominantly from vendors like **Broadcom**. A switch manufacturer typically designs the board to accommodate standard chips, like the Broadcom Jericho, which is powerful enough to store massive routing tables entirely in hardware.

Architecturally, a network device is divided into two distinct functional areas: the **Control Plane** and the **Data Plane**. The Control Plane handles logic, configuration, sensor monitoring, and protocol management, while the Data Plane acts as the "muscle," utilizing the **NPU (Network Processing Unit)** to physically process and forward the packets. These two planes are linked internally by a PCI Express bus, which generally lacks the bandwidth to allow the Control Plane CPU to process heavy network streams directly.

The Control Plane is essentially a full computer, featuring an Intel CPU (often a Celeron), RAM, and a physical storage drive. The software stack begins with the **ONI (Open Network Install Environment)**, a standard bootloader (a variant of GRUB) that loads the operating system. This open standard allows administrators to install different network operating systems, much like a regular PC. A prominent example is **SONiC**, an open-source, Linux-based network operating system initially created by Microsoft and supported by various vendors. To ensure resilience during updates, switches typically store two OS images, allowing administrators to reboot into a new version and revert safely if issues arise. The Control Plane environment is robust enough that administrators can even open a Bash shell and compile software directly on the switch.

### Switch Management and Configuration

A network switch is fundamentally a functional element: its primary duty is to **store and forward** packets reliably, yielding consistent behavior irrespective of the state of previously processed packets. Because the network acts as the central nervous system of modern IT, switches are designed for maximum resilience. If a switch reboots, it must return to its exact previous operational state without inconsistencies caused by software installations or uninstalls.

Network debugging is notoriously difficult. While the core task of moving packets is conceptually simple, the sheer volume—billions of packets per second—creates highly complex emergent behaviors. You cannot pause a live network to debug it without altering its dynamic state. For example, a degraded, but not fully broken, underground optical fiber can cause severe, unpredictable disruptions across aggregated links (like LACP) that take days to isolate. Thus, switch configurations must be pristine and predictable.

Historically, configuring a switch required an administrator to stand in a noisy data center, connected directly via a serial console cable. Today, administrators assign an IP address to the management interface (typically via an RJ45 connection to a top-of-rack management switch) and manage the device remotely via SSH. Regardless of the connection method, configuration relies on a highly efficient Command Line Interface (CLI) designed to minimize typing.

Switch CLIs utilize a modal interface, meaning available commands change depending on the current context. To streamline typing, administrators only need to type the unambiguous prefix of a command; for example, typing `conf` is interpreted as `configure`. Security is paramount—unauthorized configuration changes can disrupt entire networks—so switches utilize an **AAA (Authentication, Authorization, Audit)** model. Upon logging in, users are in a restricted mode and must type `enable` to gain privileged access, indicated by a hash symbol (`#`) in the prompt.

From the privileged mode, typing `configure` enters the configuration mode. To view the current state of the switch, the administrator uses the `show run` command, which displays the **running configuration**—the active configuration currently held in memory. If the user is currently inside a sub-context (like a specific port configuration), they must prefix the command with `do` (e.g., `do show run`) to execute a command from the parent context. To undo or remove a specific configuration, the user simply prefixes the command with `no` (e.g., `no shutdown` to activate a port).

Crucially, any changes made to the running configuration will be lost upon reboot unless they are explicitly saved to the physical disk. Administrators must issue a command like `do write` to copy the running configuration into the **startup config** file, ensuring the switch boots with the correct parameters. It is worth noting that this CLI syntax heavily mirrors the traditional Cisco operating system, which became the de-facto industry standard.

Switch ports follow a specific naming and coordinate convention, usually structured as three numbers (e.g., `Ethernet 1/1/3`). The first number represents the stack unit (allowing multiple physical switches to be managed as a single logical entity), the second is the port number, and the third is the breakout number, used when high-bandwidth ports (like QSFP) are split into multiple lower-bandwidth connections. Ports can be explicitly defined as Layer 2 `switchport` interfaces, or assigned IP addresses to function as routed interfaces.

### Virtual Local Area Networks (VLANs)

A standard Local Area Network (LAN) acts as a single broadcast domain; a packet sent to the broadcast address is received by every connected host, much like a shared bus. A **VLAN (Virtual Local Area Network)** allows administrators to logically divide a single physical network switch into multiple, isolated broadcast domains.

This is achieved by inserting a 12-bit **VLAN ID** tag into the Ethernet frame, allowing for up to 4094 distinct VLANs. The switch processes packets by filtering based on this VLAN ID. If a packet arrives on a port meant for one VLAN and is destined for another, it is strictly dropped. This logical segregation is so secure that it has replaced physical separation even in military environments, allowing distinct security classifications (e.g., confidential vs. top secret) to share the same physical cabling. Ultimately, VLANs allow data center architects to lay a fixed physical cable topology once, and dynamically redefine the network tree logically via software.

Switch ports interact with these tags in two ways:

- **Untagged Ports**: Typically connected to end-hosts (like PCs or servers). The incoming frames do not carry a VLAN tag. The switch internally tags the packet with the port's assigned default VLAN (often VLAN 1 by default), processes it, and then strips the tag off before delivering it to the destination untagged port.

- **Tagged Ports (Trunk Ports)**: Typically used for connections between switches. These ports expect packets to arrive already carrying the 12-bit VLAN tag. If an incoming packet lacks the appropriate tag, it is dropped.

- **Control Plane vs. Data Plane**: The architectural division of a network device where the Control Plane handles configuration, OS operations, and logic via a standard CPU, while the Data Plane utilizes specialized silicon (NPU) to physically forward network packets at high speeds.

- **Non-blocking Switch**: A modern switch architecture capable of processing simultaneous data transmissions across all its ports without packet delay or queuing due to internal backplane limitations.

- **Running Configuration vs. Startup Configuration**: The operational state currently active in the switch's RAM (running config) versus the persistent state saved on the disk (startup config), which loads upon reboot. Changes must be explicitly written to disk to survive a restart.

- **VLAN (Virtual Local Area Network)**: A technology that uses a 12-bit tag to logically partition a single physical network into multiple, isolated broadcast domains, enhancing security and topological flexibility.

- **PUE (Power Usage Effectiveness)**: A critical metric for data center efficiency, often improved by innovative cooling techniques like direct-to-chip liquid cooling and elevating internal rack temperatures in closed systems.ù

# Lecture 8: Fabric and Switching Continued: Advanced Network Topologies and Protocols

This section explores the evolution of data center networking, moving from basic Ethernet properties to advanced software-defined topologies. We will examine the operational mechanics of packet processing, the transition from physical VLANs to virtualized VXLAN overlays, switch management interfaces, and high-performance computing fabrics like InfiniBand.

### Ethernet in the Data Center and the MTU Concept

Ethernet has established itself as the de facto standard for local area connections and data center networks, primarily because it efficiently sustains the massive East-West traffic generated by modern aggregated computing elements. Originally designed for single coaxial cables, Ethernet's success is driven by widespread adoption and volume, despite modern switch architectures retaining very little of the original physical implementation. Over the years, the Ethernet protocol has been continuously extended to accommodate new requirements, such as altering packet headers to support virtual LANs.

At the fundamental level, network streams are split into sequences of packets, with the default Ethernet packet size being 1.5 kilobytes. The size of these packets is a critical architectural consideration. Processing every single packet requires CPU cycles; therefore, transmitting one billion tiny packets creates significantly more overhead than transmitting a smaller number of large packets. Conversely, larger packets force network switches to utilize larger, more expensive memory buffers to store, process, and forward the data.

To optimize performance, especially for heavy storage traffic like transferring large media files, the industry introduced "jumbo frames" capable of holding up to 9 kilobytes of data. The maximum allowable packet size for a device is defined by the **Maximum Transmission Unit (MTU)**. A significant challenge arises if MTU sizes are misconfigured across a network path. If a switch configured for 9-kilobyte jumbo frames sends a packet to a switch restricted to the default 1.5 kilobytes, the receiving switch will either drop the packet or fragment it into smaller pieces. This fragmentation negates the benefits of the jumbo frame and creates additional processing workload for both devices. Consequently, it is an essential network policy to ensure that the MTU remains consistent across the entirety of a single broadcast domain.

### Network Management Interfaces: CLI vs. Fat Commands

Network packet processing and configuration are managed through the control plane, traditionally utilizing a Command Line Interface (**CLI**). The CLI relies on contextual states; for example, typing a specific VLAN command enters the user into that VLAN's context, where subsequent subcommands (like `help` or `dump`) apply solely to that environment. This is conceptually identical to the behavior of the `ip` command on Linux or `netsh` on older Windows systems.

This contextual approach represents a philosophical departure from the traditional Unix model. In Unix, complex behaviors are achieved by piping together multiple small, atomic commands (e.g., `copy`, `remove`, `sort`) that perform highly specific actions. However, modern systems dealing with vast amounts of resources have largely adopted the network CLI's "fat command" philosophy. Tools like Docker encapsulate immense complexity within a single executable base command (`docker`), relying on contextual subcommands (`ps`, `exec`, `images`) to define specific functions. While this reduces the composability inherent in Unix piping, it simplifies version control and consistency, often retaining an interactive prompt to navigate contexts.

### VLANs, Trunks, and Software-Defined Topologies

A **VLAN** is fundamentally a tag added to an Ethernet frame, altering its layout to partition broadcast domains using a single physical infrastructure. When a switch port receives an untagged packet from an end host, it rewrites the packet to include the default VLAN ID assigned to that port. This allows a single 48-port switch to act as multiple isolated switches depending on how the ports are virtually assigned.

[RIFERIMENTO VISIVO DEL PROFESSORE: The speaker interacts with a CLI simulator, demonstrating how assigning specific ports (Ethernet 1 and 2) to VLAN 3 as untagged access ports isolates them from port 3, and how reassigning them alters the network topology instantly.]

End hosts, like laptops or physical servers delivering standard services, usually operate in **access mode**, generating untagged traffic. However, when routing traffic between multiple broadcast domains across different switches, utilizing a dedicated physical cable for every VLAN is highly inefficient. Instead, administrators create a **trunk**, configuring a port to accept tagged frames from multiple specific VLAN IDs.

In modern spine-and-leaf data center architectures, leaf switches are connected via trunks. If VLAN 2 is tagged on the uplink ports of multiple leaf switches and the connecting spine switch, frames from VLAN 2 can propagate seamlessly across the entire fabric. This allows the network topology to be entirely redefined via software configuration without altering the underlying physical cabling—though logical paths can only exist where physical connections are present. To ensure high availability and bandwidth, servers are typically connected with two Ethernet cables, which can be logically aggregated using LACP into a single port channel, providing 50 Gbps of active-active bandwidth on modern 25 Gbps interfaces. If the server acts as a hypervisor hosting multiple virtual machines, these links will carry tagged traffic to assign individual VMs to specific VLANs.

### VXLAN: Overcoming VLAN Limitations

While VLANs provide broadcast domain separation, they require network administrators to manually associate physical ports with specific networks. This manual intervention is unscalable for fully automated cloud providers (like AWS, Azure, or Google Cloud). Furthermore, the traditional VLAN standard is limited to roughly 4,000 IDs, which is insufficient for large-scale cloud environments.

To resolve this, the industry introduced **VXLAN**, a software-defined protocol that creates an **overlay network**. VXLAN works by encapsulating Layer 2 Ethernet frames inside Layer 3 IP packets. The physical network hardware simply routes the IP packets, remaining entirely unaware of the simulated Layer 2 networks inside them.

[RIFERIMENTO VISIVO DEL PROFESSORE: The speaker prompts an AI to generate a diagram explaining the relationship between VLAN and VXLAN while continuing the lecture.]

This encapsulation provides several massive advantages. It allows for the full automation of network topology creation without physical intervention, scales far beyond the 4,000 ID limit, and dramatically simplifies troubleshooting. Debugging Layer 2 loops is notoriously difficult because an issue can take down the entire broadcast domain without a clear source. By wrapping Layer 2 traffic in Layer 3, network engineers can utilize standard IP routing mechanics to easily track source and destination addresses, identifying the root cause of network disruptions. For example, in Azure data centers, every physical rack operates at Layer 2, but all traffic exiting the rack is encapsulated in Layer 3 via VXLAN for routing and debugging purposes.

### Switch Protocols and Quality of Service (QoS)

Beyond simple transmission, Ethernet switches implement dozens of complex protocols crammed into frame headers to manage the network, routing, and security. These include spanning tree (RSTP), tagging standards (802.1Q), and complex encapsulation methods like Q-in-Q, which nests a VLAN ID inside another VLAN ID to avoid collisions. Crucially, **EVPN** serves as the control plane for VXLAN, dictating the policies of where encapsulated traffic should be routed, a process that is increasingly hardware-accelerated directly in the switch silicon.

A common misconception is that Ethernet does not support Quality of Service (QoS). While pure Ethernet lacks inherent flow control, it has been augmented by standards like Enhanced Transmission Selection (ETS) and Priority Flow Control (PFC). PFC allows traffic to be tagged with up to 16 priority classes, partitioning bandwidth to ensure high-priority data—like storage traffic streaming to spinning disks—is never delayed.

### OpenFlow and Flow Optimization

To optimize performance, the network processing unit inside a switch relies on a flow table. Rather than fully evaluating every packet against all port configurations, the switch uses pattern matching (evaluating the source MAC, destination IP, and VLAN) to apply a set of pre-determined rules. Once a flow is established, the switch can instantly forward or drop the packet without redundant processing overhead.

Around 15 years ago, researchers proposed **OpenFlow**, an API standard that grants external software controllers direct access to modify this internal flow table. OpenFlow allows administrators to programmatically alter network topology and experiment without manually reconfiguring the switch.

[RIFERIMENTO VISIVO DEL PROFESSORE: The speaker views an AI-generated code snippet demonstrating an OpenFlow controller utilizing regular expressions to parse firewall logs and execute match-action routing commands.]

A practical application of OpenFlow is the "sandwich architecture" used to optimize Layer 7 firewalls. Layer 7 firewalls inspect the full application stream, creating a massive processing bottleneck. Using OpenFlow, an administrator can route the initial packets of a new connection through the firewall for inspection. Once the firewall log clears the connection as safe, the OpenFlow controller dynamically rewrites the flow table, rerouting the rest of the stream to bypass the firewall and travel at full switch speed. OpenFlow can also be used defensively to mirror suspicious traffic to isolated honeypots, trapping attackers without alerting them.

### Ethernet Latency and InfiniBand for HPC

Network performance is dictated by bandwidth (the size of the pipe) and latency (the time it takes for bits to become available at the destination). The hardware latency of modern Ethernet is incredibly low. Putting bits on the wire takes around 300 nanoseconds, and the switch's cut-through latency (the time it takes a packet to enter and exit) ranges between 400 and 700 nanoseconds on standard chipsets. However, the TCP/IP protocol stack—which requires sequential ordering and buffering—adds immense overhead, pushing the best-case application latency to around 70 microseconds. Thus, it is TCP, not Ethernet itself, that introduces high latency.

To solve this in High-Performance Computing (HPC) environments where nodes must constantly compute and communicate, the industry developed **InfiniBand**. InfiniBand bypasses traditional protocols, utilizing massive 2-gigabyte packets and baking software-defined functions directly into silicon to achieve application-level latencies of 1 to 2 microseconds. It relies on **RDMA (Remote Direct Memory Access)**, a technology that allows a device to transfer data across the network directly into the memory of a remote host without utilizing the remote CPU, mirroring how a local hard drive utilizes the PCIe bus.

While InfiniBand remains dominant in dedicated supercomputing data centers (often using non-oversubscribed "fat tree" topologies to prevent bandwidth degradation), Ethernet has aggressively closed the gap. The introduction of **RoCE** (RDMA over Converged Ethernet) allows standard Ethernet networks to leverage RDMA protocols, bringing InfiniBand-like low-latency communication to traditional infrastructure.

### Key Concepts

- **MTU (Maximum Transmission Unit):** The parameter defining the maximum size of a packet a network device can process. Consistency across a broadcast domain is required to prevent fragmentation and packet loss.

- **VLAN and Trunking:** VLANs use packet tagging to partition a single physical switch into multiple logical broadcast domains. Trunks allow tagged packets from multiple VLANs to travel across a single physical connection between switches.

- **VXLAN (Virtual Extensible LAN):** An overlay protocol that encapsulates Layer 2 frames within Layer 3 IP packets, enabling highly scalable, software-defined network topologies that are easier to automate and debug.

- **OpenFlow:** An API standard that allows external controllers to program a switch's internal flow table, enabling dynamic traffic routing optimizations, such as bypassing deep packet inspection firewalls for trusted streams.

- **InfiniBand and RDMA:** InfiniBand is an HPC-focused network fabric delivering ultra-low latency. It utilizes RDMA to write data directly into a remote machine's memory, bypassing CPU overhead. This capability is now available on standard networks via RoCE (RDMA over Converged Ethernet).
